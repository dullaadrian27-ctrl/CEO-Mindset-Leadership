Resource 6.1 · CEO Mindset + Leadership
CEO Identity + Quarterly Reflection
Clarify who you are as a leader, what you stand for, and where you are in your transition from operator to CEO. Do this at the start of every quarter.
Quarterly

"The biggest shift you will make in your business is not a system. It is a decision about who you are. The CEO who knows her identity makes different decisions than the one who is still figuring out if she deserves to be in the room."

— Aliya Johnson-Roberts, AJR Consulting
Part 1 — Your CEO Identity Statement
What this is for

Your CEO Identity Statement is not a bio. It is an internal declaration — the thing you say to yourself when imposter syndrome shows up, when a staff crisis makes you question your ability to lead, or when you need to remember why you built this. Write it for yourself, not for anyone else.

I am a CEO who...
I lead from...
My program exists because...
The legacy I am building is...
I know I am stepping into my CEO seat when I...
Part 2 — Where Am I in the Operator-to-CEO Transition?
The spectrum

Operator: In every room, solving every problem, unable to leave for a day without things falling apart. Doing the work of 5 people because it's easier than trusting the system.
CEO: Leading through people, systems, and strategy. Present and intentional — but not in every crisis. The business runs because of what she built, not because of where she is standing.

Delegation Framework
Figure out what only you can do, what someone else should be doing, and how to transfer it without chaos. Delegation is a skill — not a dump.
Ongoing

"Delegation is not giving someone a task and hoping they figure it out. It is transferring ownership — which means transferring the context, the authority, and the accountability. Learn the difference and your calendar changes overnight."

— Aliya Johnson-Roberts, AJR Consulting
Step 1 — Energy Audit: What's on Your Plate Right Now?
Instructions

List every recurring task or responsibility that lands on your desk. Then rate each one: Does it fill your energy or drain it? Should only you be doing it, or could someone else handle it? The answers reveal your delegation roadmap.

Step 2 — The Delegation Matrix
🔴 High Drain · Not Only Me
Delegate immediately. These are tasks draining your energy that someone else can do.
🟡 Low Drain · Not Only Me
Systematize or delegate next. Doesn't cost you much, but shouldn't be yours.
🟢 High Energy · Only Me
Protect this time. These are in your zone of genius — don't let them get crowded out.
🔵 Low Energy · Only Me
For now, these stay with you — but build toward training someone or building a system.
Step 3 — Delegation Action Plan
How to delegate properly — 5 elements

1. What: Exactly what is being transferred (be specific).
2. Why: Context — why this matters and how it connects to the bigger picture.
3. How: The process, the standard, the SOP they should follow.
4. By when: Clear deadline or recurring schedule.
5. Authority: What decisions can they make? What escalates to you?

Part 1 — Your Non-Negotiables (Name Them Before They're Negotiated Away)
What a non-negotiable actually is

A non-negotiable is not a preference. It is a boundary you hold because violating it costs you more than the short-term discomfort of holding it. Name yours now — while you are clear-headed — so you have something to point to when the pressure comes.
